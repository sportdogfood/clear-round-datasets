2. Scope of this lane (CITY/SEASON only)
--------------------------------------------------

This lane is ONLY about city + season feel. You must:

- Stay focused on:
  - How the area near the venue feels to move through in this season (light, air, pace).
  - The broader city vibe as experienced by riders, trainers, and families between show days.
  - Everyday resets (errands, food, short drives) in generic terms.

3. Research rules (CITY/SEASON)
--------------------------------------------------

You must follow the global research rules from instructions.txt (Category A/B/C, no fabrication, verification, non-breaking behavior) PLUS the city-specific rules below.

3.1 Where to look

Given:
- city, state
- lat, lng
- span_season, season_label

Follow this order:

1) Primary anchors:
   - Official city government or tourism pages for {{city}}, {{state}}.
   - County or regional tourism pages that clearly include {{city}}.
   - Pages that describe neighborhoods, parks, or everyday life in and around the lat/lng area.

2) Secondary anchors:
   - Reputable travel or encyclopedic overviews that describe:
     - Landscape/setting (farmland, coast, small town, suburbs, etc.).
     - General vibe of the city (quiet, busy, resort-like, etc.).
   - Seasonal “what it’s like in {{city}} in {{span_season}}” articles from neutral, non-spam sources.

3) Last resort:
   - Wikipedia-level overviews for {{city}} and {{state}} for:
     - Very high-level landscape and city scale.
     - Very high-level climate description (subtropical, continental, desert, etc.).

3.2 How to interpret lat / lng

- Treat lat / lng as the anchor for the competition area.
- Focus your interpretation on what it feels like within roughly a 20-mile radius around that point.
- If sources only describe the generic city center but it is clearly the same city, you may use that, but do not drift into far-flung regional features that do not match the lat/lng context.

3.3 Season and weather

- You may describe qualitative seasonal feel only:
  - Examples: “mild mornings and warm afternoons”, “humid evenings”, “crisp fall air”.
- You MUST NOT:
  - Output exact temperatures, degree symbols, or numeric ranges.
  - Guarantee weather (“always sunny”, “never cold”).
  - Contradict the given span_season or season_label.

If seasonal feel is unclear or strongly variable in sources, use "could-not-verify" instead of inventing a generic statement.

--------------------------------------------------

Field rules:

- city_research.creation_id
  - Copy from input.creation_id exactly.

- city_research.city
  - Copy from input.city (normalize to Title Case if needed, e.g. "Ocala").

- city_research.state
  - Copy from input.state (two-letter code, e.g. "FL").

- city_research.season_key
  - Copy from input.span_season (e.g. "spring").

- city_research.season_label
  - Copy from input.season_label exactly (e.g. "spring in Ocala").
  - Do NOT “correct” or rephrase this label.

- city_research.season_city_slug
  - Copy from input.season_city_slug exactly.

- city_research.weather_feel
  - 6–20 words.
  - One qualitative line about air and light in this season around the city/venue cluster.
  - No numbers, degrees, or hard guarantees.
  - Example pattern: "Mild mornings, warmer afternoons, and occasionally humid evenings during spring."
  - If you cannot verify a clear seasonal feel: "could-not-verify".

- city_research.landscape_feel
  - 6–20 words.
  - One line describing the physical setting near the venue: farmland, pine woods, low hills, coastal plain, small-town corridors, etc.

  - If you cannot verify: "could-not-verify".

- city_research.reset_feel
  - 8–25 words.
  - How riders, trainers, and families typically “reset” around town in generic terms:

    - simple meals,

  - Do NOT name specific businesses.
  - If you cannot verify: "could-not-verify".
